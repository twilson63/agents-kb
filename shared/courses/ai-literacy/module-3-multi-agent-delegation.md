# Module 3: Multi-Agent & Delegation

**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Goal:** Students leave with the mental model for thinking in agent teams — knowing when to delegate, how to structure handoffs, and how to supervise without micromanaging.

---

## Essay 1 — Why One Agent Can't Do Everything Well

**Core idea:** Asking a single agent to handle a complex, multi-step project produces mediocre results across the board. The same way one person can't be a researcher, writer, reviewer, and project manager simultaneously, neither can one agent.

**Outline:**
- The "do everything" trap: when one prompt tries to cover too much
- How quality degrades when an agent context-switches too much
- The human analogy: specialists vs. generalists, and when each is right
- What multi-agent means in practice: different agents with different focuses working together
- The shift in mindset: from "what should I ask?" to "who should handle this?"

**Pod exercise:** Student identifies a complex task from their work and lists the distinct roles involved (researcher, drafter, reviewer, etc.). This becomes the blueprint for their first delegation exercise.

---

## Essay 2 — Thinking in Roles

**Core idea:** Before you can delegate, you need to think about what roles a task requires. This essay teaches students to decompose work into distinct responsibilities.

**Outline:**
- Common agent roles: researcher, writer, editor, reviewer, planner, executor
- How to identify roles from a task: what are the distinct phases? What expertise does each need?
- The importance of clear role boundaries — what each agent is and isn't responsible for
- Roles don't have to be fancy: even "first draft" and "final review" is a two-role workflow
- Practice: mapping familiar work tasks to roles

**Pod exercise:** Student takes their task from Essay 1 and writes a one-sentence role description for each agent they'd use. What is each one responsible for? What is it NOT responsible for?

---

## Essay 3 — Your First Delegation

**Core idea:** The simplest form of delegation is asking one agent to research something, then handing that research to a second agent to do something with it. This essay walks through that first handoff.

**Outline:**
- The two-agent pattern: researcher → writer (or any producer → consumer)
- How to hand off: summarizing one agent's output as the input for the next
- What makes a good handoff: complete, specific, no ambiguity
- Common first delegations: research + summarize, draft + review, plan + execute
- The feeling of leverage: what it's like when the second agent benefits from the first's work

**Pod exercise:** Student runs a two-agent workflow in their practice pod. Agent 1 researches a topic. Student reviews the output, then passes it to Agent 2 with a clear brief. Agent 2 produces a final output.

---

## Essay 4 — Parallel Work vs. Sequential Work

**Core idea:** Some agent workflows run one step at a time. Others can run simultaneously. Knowing the difference lets you use agent teams much more efficiently.

**Outline:**
- Sequential work: each step depends on the previous one (research → write → review)
- Parallel work: steps that can happen at the same time (research three topics simultaneously)
- How to spot opportunities for parallel work in your own workflows
- The leverage of parallel: what takes one agent an hour can take multiple agents minutes
- Practical example: preparing for a meeting where you need research on three different angles

**Pod exercise:** Student identifies one workflow from their work that could have parallel steps. They run both parallel tasks in their pod and compare the time savings.

---

## Essay 5 — How Agents Hand Off to Each Other

**Core idea:** A smooth handoff is the difference between a coherent multi-agent workflow and a disconnected mess. This essay covers the mechanics and principles of good handoffs.

**Outline:**
- What a handoff actually is: a summary + a brief + a clear ask
- The three parts of a good handoff: what happened, what it means, what you need next
- Common handoff failures: too much information, too little context, unclear ask
- The human analogy: handing off a project to a colleague — what would you tell them?
- Templates: simple handoff format students can use in their pods

**Pod exercise:** Student writes a formal handoff document from one agent's output to the next, using the template. Run the receiving agent with that handoff and evaluate the result.

---

## Essay 6 — When to Supervise vs. When to Trust

**Core idea:** One of the hardest things about delegation — with humans or agents — is knowing when to check in and when to let them run. This essay gives students a practical framework.

**Outline:**
- The micromanagement trap: checking every step kills the value of delegation
- The trust trap: not checking at all leads to compounding errors
- How to calibrate: task complexity, agent familiarity, stakes of getting it wrong
- Supervision checkpoints: where to review without babysitting
- The correction instinct: when an agent goes off course, how to redirect without starting over

**Pod exercise:** Student runs a three-step workflow and decides in advance where their supervision checkpoint is. After the workflow, evaluate: was the checkpoint in the right place?

---

## Essay 7 — The Future You're Preparing For

**Core idea:** A closing essay that connects everything in the course to the bigger picture — why AI literacy matters now, what the agent-driven world looks like, and what students are ready for.

**Outline:**
- Where AI agents are headed: more capable, more autonomous, more integrated into everyday work
- The people who will thrive: those who can think in terms of agents, context, delegation, and workflows
- What this course has built: not just skills, but a mental model for working with AI
- The compounding effect: every skill you write, every memory note you add, every workflow you build makes you more capable
- The invitation: keep building — your practice pod is yours to experiment in

**Pod exercise:** Student writes a 30-day plan: three memory notes to add, two skills to build, one multi-agent workflow to try. Share with the group as a commitment.

---

## Instructor Notes

- The human analogy (delegation to colleagues) is the most effective frame for non-technical audiences — use it consistently
- Many students are surprised by parallel work — the "simultaneous" concept is counterintuitive for people used to linear workflows
- Essay 7 should feel inspiring, not overwhelming — the goal is confidence, not information overload
- The 30-day plan exercise at the end converts learning into commitment — give it real time
- Close by reminding students that their practice pod stays with them — it's not a classroom exercise, it's the start of their actual workflow
