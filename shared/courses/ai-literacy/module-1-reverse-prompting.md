# Module 1: Reverse Prompting

**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Goal:** Students leave knowing how to have a productive conversation with an AI agent instead of issuing commands and getting frustrated when results miss the mark.

---

## Essay 1 — Why Your First Prompt Is Usually Wrong

**Core idea:** Most people treat AI like a vending machine. You put in a request, expect a result. But the first prompt almost never has enough context. This isn't the AI failing — it's a mental model problem.

**Outline:**
- The vending machine instinct: one input, one output
- What the agent actually needs to do its job well
- Why vague requests produce generic results
- The shift: prompting is the beginning of a conversation, not the whole conversation
- Preview: what reverse prompting changes

**Pod exercise:** Student sends a vague prompt to their pod ("help me write an email") and notes what comes back. Then sends a context-rich version. Compare the two outputs.

---

## Essay 2 — The Conversation Mindset

**Core idea:** The most productive way to work with an agent is to treat it as a thought partner you're briefing, not a tool you're operating.

**Outline:**
- What it means to "brief" someone vs. "command" someone
- Agents work best when they understand the why, not just the what
- You don't need to know the answer — the agent helps you find it
- Analogy: working with a smart new colleague who knows nothing about your context yet
- The goal is a shared understanding, not a perfect first output

**Pod exercise:** Student picks a real task they need to do. Instead of prompting for the output, they write a message explaining the situation to the agent as if talking to a colleague.

---

## Essay 3 — Ask the Agent to Ask You Questions

**Core idea:** One of the most powerful prompting techniques is telling the agent you want it to interview you before it starts working. This is reverse prompting — you flip the dynamic.

**Outline:**
- The technique: "Before you respond, ask me the questions you need to do this well"
- Why this works: agents know what information they need, they just don't ask by default
- Example: planning a project, writing a document, making a decision
- The result: a much better output because the agent had what it needed
- When to use it (complex tasks) vs. when to skip it (simple tasks)

**Pod exercise:** Student uses the exact phrase "Before you respond, ask me everything you need to know to help me with [task]." Run the conversation through to a result.

---

## Essay 4 — Context Before Commands

**Core idea:** The most common prompting mistake is skipping straight to the ask. Giving context first — who you are, what you're trying to accomplish, what constraints matter — changes everything.

**Outline:**
- What context actually means: role, goal, constraints, tone, audience
- The difference between "write a bio" and "write a bio for a LinkedIn profile for a non-technical product manager applying for senior roles"
- Context isn't just about the task — it's about you and your situation
- How to build a context habit: start every session with a quick briefing
- You can save context as memory so you don't repeat it every time (preview for Module 2)

**Pod exercise:** Student writes a "personal context card" — 3-5 sentences about who they are and how they work — and pastes it at the start of a prompt. Compare the result with and without it.

---

## Essay 5 — Iteration: Refining vs. Starting Over

**Core idea:** When an output isn't right, most people start over with a new prompt. Usually that's wrong. The better move is to tell the agent what missed and keep the conversation going.

**Outline:**
- Why starting over loses valuable context
- How to give feedback that actually helps: specific, directional, non-destructive
- "That's not quite right because..." vs. "Make it better"
- When iteration works and when starting fresh is actually right (tone mismatch, wrong direction entirely)
- Building toward the output you want through multiple rounds

**Pod exercise:** Student takes a mediocre output from a previous exercise and iterates on it with three rounds of specific feedback. Goal: reach a result they'd actually use.

---

## Essay 6 — Reading the Agent's Response

**Core idea:** The agent's output contains signals about how well it understood you. Learning to read those signals helps you improve your next prompt.

**Outline:**
- What to look for: hedging language, generic phrasing, missed constraints
- When the agent asks clarifying questions — that's a good sign, not a failure
- Spotting when the agent has misunderstood your intent
- Confidence vs. uncertainty in outputs
- Using the agent's uncertainty as a guide for your next message

**Pod exercise:** Student reviews an output and annotates it: highlight where the agent was confident, where it hedged, and what it got wrong. Then craft the next message based on those signals.

---

## Essay 7 — Breaking Big Requests into Conversations

**Core idea:** Asking an agent to do too much at once produces bloated, unfocused results. Breaking complex work into a series of focused conversations gets better outputs at every step.

**Outline:**
- The "one prompt to rule them all" trap
- How to decompose a big task: what are the natural stages?
- Each stage as its own conversation with its own context
- Carrying forward: summarizing one stage to brief the next
- When to combine steps vs. when to keep them separate

**Pod exercise:** Student takes a large task ("plan a team offsite") and maps it into 4-5 separate conversations. Run the first two conversations in their pod.

---

## Essay 8 — Building Your Prompting Instincts

**Core idea:** Good prompting isn't a formula — it's a set of instincts you develop through practice. This essay consolidates everything and gives students a personal checklist they'll actually use.

**Outline:**
- The five-second check before you send any prompt: context, goal, constraints, format, audience
- When to use reverse prompting (ask it to ask you)
- When to iterate vs. start over
- The habit of saving prompts that worked
- Where to go from here: skills and memory (preview for Module 2)

**Pod exercise:** Student runs a real work task end-to-end using everything from Module 1. Debrief: what worked, what they'd do differently.

---

## Instructor Notes

- The vending machine analogy lands well for non-technical audiences — use it early and return to it
- Essay 3 (reverse prompting technique) is the most surprising to students — give it extra time
- Practice pod exercises should use real tasks from the student's actual work where possible
- The module ends on a forward-looking note: habits from Module 1 are the foundation for everything in Modules 2 and 3
